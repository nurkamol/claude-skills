Why Roseville Projects Require HERS Field Verification

Roseville falls under Placer County's building jurisdiction, which enforces California's Title 24 Building Energy Efficiency Standards for all permitted HVAC work — new construction and alterations alike. Title 24 doesn't just require that systems be designed to meet efficiency targets. It requires that they actually perform to those standards after installation.

That's where the HERS rater comes in. A certified third-party rater performs on-site diagnostic tests and submits the results to the official HERS registry before the building department will final the permit. No HERS report, no permit closure — regardless of how well the installation was done.

What Title 24 Actually Requires

California's energy code governs everything from duct leakage rates and refrigerant charge accuracy to air handler fan efficiency and insulation installation quality. The specific measures required for any given Roseville project are listed on the CF-1R compliance form submitted at permit application.

Reviewing that CF-1R before installation begins is the single most important step a Roseville contractor can take. It identifies every HERS measure the project must pass — and lets your team plan the installation around hitting those targets from the start rather than scrambling to correct issues after the fact. You can find a full overview of California's energy code standards at the California Energy Commission's Title 24 program page.

5 Permit Situations That Trigger HERS Testing in Roseville

1. Full HVAC System Replacement

Replacing both the air handler and condenser — along with associated ductwork — almost always triggers duct leakage testing and refrigerant charge verification at minimum. This is the most common scenario Express Duct Test handles for Roseville contractors.

2. New Duct System Installation

Any permitted project involving new ductwork requires a duct leakage test confirming the system meets California's thresholds. For new Roseville construction, that standard is 4% leakage or less of total system airflow — a tight target that demands careful sealing during installation.

3. Air Handler Replacement Only

Replacing just the indoor air handler — without touching the condenser or full duct system — can still trigger HERS measures if the CF-1R specifies them. Roseville contractors often miss this in repair scenarios. Always confirm the compliance form before assuming a partial replacement is measure-free.

4. Room Additions With HVAC Extension

Expanding conditioned space and extending the existing duct system typically triggers duct leakage testing on the modified system — and potentially refrigerant charge and airflow verification depending on scope.

5. Permitted Insulation Projects

Attic re-insulation projects that are permit-required may trigger an HERS insulation installation quality inspection — confirming that R-value and installation method match the permit specifications.

What Field Verification Involves

The tests performed during a HERS visit depend on which measures appear on the CF-1R. For most Roseville residential HVAC projects, these are the most common:

  • Duct Leakage Testing — the duct system is pressurized and leakage is measured as a percentage of rated airflow. Results are submitted to the HERS registry.
  • Refrigerant Charge Verification — confirms charge is within the manufacturer's specified range using calibrated measurement equipment.
  • Airflow Measurement — verifies actual airflow across the evaporator coil matches rated specifications.
  • Fan Efficacy Verification — confirms the air handler fan operates within California's required watts-per-CFM range, a requirement introduced in the 2019 and 2022 code cycles.

How to Pass Your HERS Test the First Time

Failed HERS tests add cost, time, and frustration to any Roseville project. These steps virtually eliminate first-time failures:

  • Review the CF-1R before installation begins — know every required measure upfront
  • Seal all duct connections with mastic during installation, not after
  • Verify refrigerant charge with calibrated equipment and document the result before the rater arrives
  • Confirm airflow matches the equipment's rated specifications
  • Ensure attic access is clear and safe for the rater to inspect duct sections
  • Schedule the HERS visit as part of the project timeline — not as a last-minute call
